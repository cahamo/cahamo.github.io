Electro-Tongue Drum Project

Introduction

This is a project to build an electronic emulation of a Tongue Drum, also known as the tank drum or a hank drum.

I've described elsewhere what my motivation is to build this project. See my post “My 1st Project - Say Hello to the ‘Electro Tongue Drum’” in my blog for more information.

Project Brief

The main features of the electro-tongue drum should be:

  • The shape of the instrument will echo that of the acoustic instrument, but will not copy it slavishly. Think of the difference between an acoustic and electric guitar. This intention is that the instrument will be circular or polygonal in plan view, but will be relatively flat. It is not desirable to introduce a large chamber that could amplify the percusive sound of fingers hitting the “tongues” *.
  • The instrument will be operated by means of touch sensitve pads, rather than mechanical keys, laid out in a similar pattern to a tongue drum’s tongues. This layout to make the transition between the acoustic instrument and this electronic version as seemless as possible. Unfortunatley tongue drums come with varying numbers of tongues, so a decision needs to be taken how many the project instrument will have.
  • Ideally there should be two modes of operation:
    1. Audio Mode where the instrument produces sound when a key is pressed. The sound may be reproduced via an internal speaker and/or it may be output via a line level audio output or to headphones. A decision is required as to whether the sound is synthesised or is made by triggering samples of an aoustic instrument.
    2. MIDI Mode where the instrument outputs suitable MIDI messages is response to tongue hits. Such messages will be interprested by software that can use the notes to drive MIDI instruments. In particular, the notes should be compatible with tongue drum virtual instruments. Such compatibility may require that several different user configurable MIDI note mappings are provided.
  • The instrument should be capabable of stand alone operation, which implies that it must have Audio Mode and be able to be powered usuing batteries.

* – A “tongue” on a tongue drum is the area that the player strikes to create a note. This is analgous to a key on a keyboard.

Progress

At present this is a very new project and I am very inexperienced, having just begun to learn electronics in April of 2021.

The project is very much in the experimental phase. Having determined that the touch sensitive “tongues” are to use capacitive sensing, early experiments are being conducted to learn about suitable material and software.

Experiments

To date, these are the experiments that have been conducted:

#1: Graphite sensor

Tests the responsiveness of a single graphite sensor using the CapacitiveSensor library with various sample sizes.

#2: Tin foil sensor

Tests the responsiveness of a single tin foil sensor using the CapacitiveSensor library with various sample sizes.

#3: Comparison of different types of tin foil sensors

Compares the effectiveness of different qualities of tin foil sensors.

#4: Comparison of different sensor coverings

Compares the impact on sensor readings by covering a tin foil snesor with various materials.

#5: Comparison of different send pin resistances

Compares the impact on sensor readings of various resistances between the detector’s send pin and the sensor.

Design Decisions

Number of tongues

Research has indicated that both eight and nine key tongue drums are popular, so it has been decided to make the electro-tongue drum a nine key instrument.

The nine key tongue drum has a central tongue and eight outer tongues around the circumference of the instrument. The following image illustrates the layout:

The eight key tongue drum has the same layout as the nine key version, except that the centre tongue is omitted. In a later version it is proposed to offer an option to switch the instrument into eight key mode by disabling the centre tongue.

Note order

The notes will be laid out in accordance with standard practice.

To illustrate let us label the outer tongues according to their compass positions. The lowest note is on the centre tongue, then the notes increase in pitch is this order: south, south-west, south-east, west, east, north-west, north-east and finally north.

The following diagram show the notes labelled in ascending pitch order from 1 to 9.

Notice that no scale has been specified here, only the order in which pitch ascends (and by inference descends) across the tongues. The matter of scale and tuning will be addressed next.

Tunings

Acoustic tongue drums are tuned to a pre-determined key. Some manufacturers offer their instruments in a choice of keys. Some tongue drums can be re-tuned.

Since the electro-tongue drum will generate its notes artificially, there is no reason why a number of scales can't be supported.

Here's a possible roadmap identifying how such a feature may be added, depending on mode:

Stage † Audio Mode MIDI Mode
1 A single scale will be provided. A fixed scale of MIDI notes will be provided, for example C major ‡.
2 A choice of up to 8 pre-determined scales will be provided, along with a means of selecting the required scale. The first scale will be the same as the fixed scale provided at stage 1.
3 The user will be given the option of defining their own scales. These will be uploaded to the instrument either by SD card or USB. It is likely that there will be a limit of uploading no more than eight scales at a time.
The notes available for use will be restricted by the number of samples available. The notes available will be un-restricted. However, the user will need to limit the scales to those that can be realistically played by on the available virtual instruments.

† – The different modes may not reach the same stage of development at the same time. For example it is looking likely that a MIDI instrument will be created before Audio Mode is developed.

‡ – This is not as restrictive as it first appears since some Digital Audio Workstations can translate inputted MIDI notes into other notes, meaning a scale can be translated into another scale before arriving at the virtual instrument. Relevant mappings may be provided here.

It is proposed that the first version of the instrument that has an Audio Mode (see above) will provide a choice of up to eight pre-defined scales. A later version may offer the ability for the user to define their own scales, possibly via an SSD card or USB port. An accompanying app may be made available to assist with creating such scales.

When considering MIDI mode, it is easy for the instrument to be used to generate any eight or nine note scale. In the first version to have MIDI Mode> it is proposed to output arbitrary MIDI note numbers, for example C major. Many DAWs will have the ability to transpose incoming MIDI notes into any sequence desired by the user, so that the notes be sent by the electro-tongue drum can be interpreted as any scale. Later versions may provide the the user with ability to select certain scales and output the correct MIDI notes for them, thus saving the user from having to set up a translation in their DAW. As with Audio Mode, the facility to upload different scales may be provided.
